The Perfect Aspect

A bridge between two points in time. Three faces of the same form: resultative, experiential, continuative.

Level: B2 / C1 Sections: 7 Focus: Present & Past Perfect

What is the perfect aspect?

The perfect aspect signals that an action, event, or state happened before some reference point on the timeline, and that this earlier event still matters for that point. The reference point is usually the present (present perfect) or another moment in the past (past perfect). The Latin root perfectum means "completed", but the form does more than mark completion: it builds a bridge between then and now.

If the bridge is missing (no real connection between the earlier event and the later moment), what you have is not the perfect aspect at all. It is a simple form. This is the line that divides I lost my keys (a fact reported about the past) from I have lost my keys (a current state of being key-less, caused by an earlier loss).

A note on terminology
Typological literature (Bybee, Dahl) sometimes uses anterior as a cover term for the entire perfect aspect, precisely because its core function is locating an event before a reference point with a connection between them. Older works split off a separate "anterior of recent past" inside the perfect, but most modern accounts (Iatridou, Pancheva, Mittwoch) fold it into the resultative reading. You will meet that older split in some textbooks. It is not wrong, just outdated.

A second note: this worksheet uses three readings (resultative, experiential, continuative), following Huddleston & Pullum's Cambridge Grammar of the English Language. Other sources draw the lines differently: Comrie (1976) lists four (adding "recent past"), Wikipedia lists five, Iatridou et al. (2001) collapse them into two (universal vs existential, where their universal is what we call continuative). The three-way split is a pedagogical choice that maps cleanly onto the diagnostics, not the only possible carving.

I. Resultative perfect
Refers to a state at the reference point that is the direct consequence of an earlier event. The focus is on the lingering result, not on the event itself. If the result no longer holds, the sentence stops being appropriate.
I have lost my keys. · result: I do not have them now.
She has broken her leg. · result: her leg is in a cast.
It has rained. · result: the streets are wet.

II. Experiential perfect
States that a situation has occurred at least once in the period up to the reference point. The whole past is treated as an open window. Focus is on the bare fact of the experience, not when it happened or what state it left.
I have been to Japan. · at least once in my life.
Have you ever read Ulysses?
She has visited Rome three times.
Cue · ever, never, X times

III. Continuative perfect
A situation that started in the past and continues without interruption up to the reference point (and often beyond). Also called perfect of persistent situation (Comrie) or universal perfect (Iatridou et al.). Same category, different names.
I have lived here for 10 years. · still living here.
She has worked at the bank since 1997.
We have known each other our whole lives.

§ 07

The anterior trap

Some reference sources (including the current Wikipedia entry on the perfect, and earlier descriptive grammars rooted in Comrie 1976) split the perfect into a resultative reading and a separate anterior of recent past, treating the second as "weaker": no specific result state, just a closed action with general relevance. The example often given was It has rained as anterior, but I have lost my keys as resultative. Newer accounts disagree.

Why the split is artificial

If the overarching definition of the perfect aspect already requires a connection between the earlier event and the reference point, then every present perfect by definition has some connection. Carving out a category called "connection, but weaker" duplicates work the aspect itself already does.

That is why current literature folds the older "anterior" reading into the resultative reading, and reserves the term anterior for the perfect aspect as a whole, not a sub-reading inside it.

§ Sources

References and further reading

The terminology and the three-way classification used in this worksheet draw on the following sources. Where authors disagree (and they do), the choices made here lean on Huddleston & Pullum and Mittwoch.

  1. Comrie, Bernard (1976). Aspect: An Introduction to the Study of Verbal Aspect and Related Problems. Cambridge University Press. The classic four-way split: perfect of result, experiential perfect, perfect of persistent situation, perfect of recent past. Still widely cited.
  2. Bybee, Joan, Revere Perkins & William Pagliuca (1994). The Evolution of Grammar: Tense, Aspect, and Modality in the Languages of the World. University of Chicago Press. Source of the typological term anterior as a cover label for the perfect aspect across languages.
  3. Iatridou, Sabine, Elena Anagnostopoulou & Roumyana Izvorski (2001). Observations about the form and meaning of the perfect. In Michael Kenstowicz (ed.), Ken Hale: A Life in Language, MIT Press, 189-238. Two-way universal vs existential split; their universal matches what this worksheet calls continuative.
  4. Huddleston, Rodney & Geoffrey K. Pullum (2002). The Cambridge Grammar of the English Language. Cambridge University Press. The descriptive grammar that uses the term continuative perfect; primary source of the three-way split adopted here.
  5. Pancheva, Roumyana (2003). The aspectual makeup of perfect participles and the interpretations of the perfect. In Alexiadou, Rathert & von Stechow (eds.), Perfect Explorations, Mouton de Gruyter, 277-306. Argues for grouping resultative and experiential under a single existential perfect, contrasted with the universal reading.
  6. Mittwoch, Anita (2008). The English Resultative Perfect and Its Relationship to the Experiential Perfect and the Simple Past Tense. Linguistics and Philosophy 31(3): 323-351. The argument for folding Comrie's perfect of recent past into the resultative reading.

A reminder: every author cited here is reading the same English data, but cuts the cake differently. The three-way split used in this worksheet is the most pedagogically tractable, not a settled metaphysical fact about the language.
